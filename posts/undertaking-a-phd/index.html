Considering a PhD? A guide based on personal experience

My own perspective on undertaking a PhD in physics, adressed to those who might be deciding about starting a PhD programme.

Prior to starting my PhD back in 2019, I remember spending long evenings on Google, Quora and Reddit, looking for people’s own opinions and perspectives on the big life decision I was facing in the time: “Should I go to grad school and start a PhD programme?”. At that time, I was just about to finish my Masters degree in Nanotechnology back in Czechia, and have also just return from nearly a year-long stint at ICFO (Institut de Ciències Fotòniques) in Catalunya, Spain, where I did a Masters project.

Up to that point, my education was certainly directing me towards being a researcher. My colleagues at ICFO have mentioned on multiple occasions that they think I would be “a good fit” for a PhD programme. The prospects of earning a somewhat more rare, advanced academic degree were certainly tempting (even if only as a personal challenge). In adition, after earning both my Bc. and Ing. degrees in Czechia, the prospects of having an internationally well-recognized academic degree were quite enticing as well.

Over four years later, I am now reaching the very final weeks of my PhD. Looking back, I would like to write this article and address it to my old self, 25 yeard old Matěj being uncertain and indecisive about the big choice of starting the PhD - and perhaps more importantly, I would like to address is this to anyone who might find themselves in the same position as I was. Hopefully, this will be helpful to someone! 😊 If you have any further questions, please do not hesitate to reach out!

To preface, I would like to clearly state that my perspective will come from undertaking a PhD in Physics, one of the “hard” sciences/STEM fields, and from undertaking my PhD in the UK, with some general insight into the process from other European countries as well (based on experience of my friends).

First, I will try to provide some guidance on the process of choosing a PhD programme based on a personal experience with the system. Later, I will provide some personal perspective on the value (and challenges) of doing a PhD.

Step 1: Understand the system & the conditions¶

Different countries allow for different progressions through one’s studies. Back in Czechia, a PhD only follows after a completed Bachelors + Masters (3+2 years). Sometimes, a year of studies can be “saved” by combining places - I know people who did Bc. in Czechia, followed by one-year masters in the Netherlands or Spain. While I still don’t completely understand the UK system with their Honours degrees etc., from my perspectives, a PhD can be started (on average) at comparatively younger age than back home in Czechia. Furthermore, the American system differs significantly as well, where a PhD can be started after an undergrad, and transferred to “only” a Masters halfway through, if preferred. In Europe, PhD projects are typically intended to take between 3.5-4 years, and very often take longer via project extensions.

Hand in hand with this goes the awareness of overall conditions for PhD students in the country of choice, as these can again differ significantly. These conditons include, naturally, the form of compensation for the student, who typically works as a junior researcher/scientist and devotes to their PhD in a form equivalent (or even exceeding) that of a full-time employment. PhD projects are typically funded, although self-funded projects exist as well (AFAIK this is very rare in Physics and other technical fields, at least from my experience).

The compensation and working conditions (again) differ significantly between countries: in most cases, despite typically functioning as a full-time worker at a given institution, PhDs are not considered as employees and are compensated with tax-exempt stipends. The non-taxed character of these stipends typically mean no contributions to social security etc., and therefore brings certain disadvantages such as lack of well defined common worker rights (maternity leaves etc.). An exemption to this is for example in Scandinavian countries, where PhDs are considered as employees (in my opinion, this should be the case everywhere).

The wage for a funded PhD student is (according to the systems I am aware of) usually set on a nation-wide level. In the UK, this is (as of March 2023) equivalent to £17,668/year. This is tax-free (netto) wage, paid to the student in monthly increments. This figure increases by few percent every year between August and September. Also, I believe this figure is higher for students in London. Certain schemes (such as Engineering Doctorates, EngD) include higher compensation coming from a company contribution.

For teaching activities here in Scotland, I was (personally) always paid extra on per-hour basis. In some countries, involvement in teaching activities (such as lab demonstrator) is mandatory for a PhD. Also, in some countries, mandatory coursework represents part of a PhD. Undertaking coursework is typically not mandatory case in the UK, but might be the case given individual circumstances (such as mandatory Physics courses by SUPA at Scottish universities).

In short summary, whichever country you are considering for your PhD, it’s worth it to get a good understanding of the local conditions for PhD studentships. As these might also have various university-specific adjustments, it’s a good idea to ask someone undertaking the PhD at your university of choice (if you don’t know anyone, try searching or asking on Reddit). You are likely to be paid fairly low wage in comparison to (technical) industry jobs, so consider if that is okay for your personal circumstances and situation.

Step 2: Choose project & group carefully¶

As I have previously mentioned, your PhD studies will take at minimum ~4 years. In more than one case, I have witnessed people who ended up starting their PhDs after their Masters purely based on momentum - they did not want to leave the familiar environment of their university, wanted to keep living the student life and not start with a “boring” job right away. In my perspective, this is not what I would consider a well-based decision.

My perspective: don’t do a PhD solely just because you want those three letters behind your name. In such case (where this would be the sole and main motivation), the long & tedious character of the PhD journey will likely make you hate your life, and you will also likely drop-out before finishing. Instead, consider it as a chance to satiate your curiosity and work on an interesting problem with lots of personal freedom, in an environment that faciliates learning and personal growth, while already having some personal financial security thanks to the stipend. If you are succesful in this journey, you will gain a few extra letters to your name, which will ultimately represent the least significant benefit of the whole journey. 😉 Sounds fair? Okay, let’s proceed.

Now, you have to choose your project.

First question I would ask myself when choosing a PhD project would be:

Is this a “fuck yes” project proposal/idea/field in my eyes? Does the idea, or the field, or the potential results intrigue or excite me to the point where I can (at this point) imagine working on this single problem for 4 years of my life, constantly dealing with hardships and complications innevitably coming with any research endeavour?

(Here, I have to thank Mark Manson for his “fuck yes or nothing” approach, which I like to rely on in such situations.)

This might sound too harsh/restrictive, and perhaps it is - but for me, this is a good litmus test to manage expectations. After working on my PhD for almost four years at this moment, I still find my research field really cool and exciting - and I believe this fascination by the promise and the idea was what helped me achieve a good degree of long-term self-motivation, drive as well as good amount of research outputs. Unlike the preceeding degrees which were primarily about assignments and following coursework, PhD is significantly more about personal independence and self-motivation to lead on the activities of the research project. Without self-initiative, a PhD can be a really mentally painful journey with very uncertain prospects for finishing.

In addition, you might be considering a PhD in a field not related to the field in which you did your undergraduate and/or graduate studies. Perhaps you realized that those previous studies were not what you were interested in, or maybe you picked up a new passion project that changed your perspective on what you truly enjoy working on. I personally believe that changing direction for your PhD is perfectly fine, and **shouldn’t **be frowned upon or considered wrong. Your prospective supervisor might be perhaps a bit hesitant to consider a candidate from a vastly different background, but if you communicate your motivation and decision well, I believe your chance for being accepted for the programme will be on par with that of other candidates.

Second question which closely relates to the previous one, and which I find equally (if not even more) important is:

Your supervisor will be your closest point of contact for your activities for years to come.

I have a personal saying: “A supervisor during your PhD can either make you or break you.”

I have witnessed both cases: in my case, I was extremely fortunate to work with a supervisor who provided a great, dynamical working environment that fits my personal drive and workstyle, who gave me opportunities to interact globally with many members of our research fields, and who I believe truly helped me flourish and get the most of my PhD (shoutout here to Antonio! 👋). On the other hand, I have also witnessed less ideal situations. For instance, one of my friends supervisor was a toxic, somewhat dismissive of people based on their gender and very dismissive of people coming from particular regions, and all-together was a very mentally taxing person to deal with, all while radiating the “best person ever” aura to the people outside of the research group.

Obviously, it can be very difficult (or even straight-out impossible) to properly judge a persons character from a short interaction prior to starting a project, especially if the project is started remotely (for example when moving for a PhD) and when interaction involves only online meetings and emails. I would say: the least one can do is to trust your gut, and trust your instincts. What does the interaction with the person feel like? If you feel like there are some red flags, don’t be dismissive. Furthermore, a prospective supervisor should in my opinion be perfectly fine to let you chat with current group members - and most importantly, current PhD students - on how things are going in the research group. These people will know the circumstances better than anyone, and I would strongly recommend having a casual chat and ask for their honest opinion, all while using private communication circumstances or channels (i.e., not while the supervisor is watching over their shoulder on a group Zoom call, for example). Keep in mind, some people can have personal biases that might not perfectly reflect the actual reality - but nonetheless, having a chat with more than one person should provide a good overall understanding of the situation within the research group.

I would also like to add one additional personal remark here related to the topic of prestige of research institutions. It wouldn’t be honest to say that credentialism does not exist - if you will earn a PhD from an Ivy League school such as Harvard or Stanford, or another prestigious place such as ETH Zürich, it will very likely be benefitial for your future career prospects. One might dislike it, but it’s necessary to acknowledge this as that’s how we (as people, as society) think. Nonetheless, what I would like to raise here is that I would **not **restrict oneself to only be interested in PhD from prestigious institutions. For doctoral degrees in particular, the research group you are joining is what matters the most - and there’s many, many cases of world leading teams in their respective domains of expertise that operate from smaller or less famous institutions. It is true that the likelihood of doing a true cutting-edge, state-of-the-art research is higher at prestigious institutions (simply because these places attract the most capable and ambitious people, and are also typically awash with research funds, resources and grants). However, that certainly does not mean that all research outside of the prestigious places is of subpar quality - very much on the contrary! Therefore, I would most certainly not hesitate to include institutions from broader range of rankings when considering PhD projects - the specific team is what matters more than the prestige of the institution.

Okay! So, you now understand the circumstances of doing a PhD in your country of choice ✅, you found an exciting PhD project ✅ and the supervisor feels like a genuinely good person to work with ✅, which is supported by experience of current group members? ✅ Then, I think you are in a perfect spot to start your PhD journey. 😊

Step 3: Y’er a PhD student now, Harry.¶

With all the stars well aligned for your project, you have been accepted to your selected research group and have now started your PhD. Congratulations!

Probably the most important thing to realize is that this is, more than anything, a marathon rather than a sprint. At this moment, it might not feel tangible how you will achieve the grand goals of your project, and how will you manage to contribute to this advanced research field. That’s okay - take a deep breath, and get started with the small things first. Your first months are intended to get you started and involved with current group activities, and more importantly, to give you time to get yourself up-to-date on the state of the art within your research domain. As mentioned before, the beauty of the PhD is that your compensation (stipend) is not directly dependent on investing your time only into activities with immediately clear value proposition. In other words, you can spend weeks literally just reading and learning about things that fascinate you - this type of intelectual freedom is arguably one of the greatest pleasures of doing a doctorate.

While you study the latest publications in your field, use a reference manager such as Zotero (strongly recommend this particular software among the available options) to keep track of your literature, and to aid you with future writing activities. Between six to twelve months into your project, you should have a good general understanding of the field and lab activites, and it might be a good idea to create a general work plan for the remainder of your studies, to make sure your research activities all fit within a frame and provide the necessary support and evidence for your thesis you will write at the end.

In the meantime, strive from the beginning to create a clear, well defined data storage plan or hiearchy (this will end up being a process, but try to adopt good practice from the very beginning). Ideally, aim to store your digital data in a future-proof way and open formats. Always take good notes and documentation of all the experiments and studies you undertake, as there’s (from my experience) no greater lie than “I will remember this obvious, small detail from the experiment.” My personal tip: put timestaps on everything you create, preferably in a YYYY-MM-DD format, which automatically sort things chronologically by name. Oh, and try to avoid comparing your PhD project and PhD progress to the other members of your cohort or department. Unlike in the case of undergraduate studies, where everyone is handed the same exam sheet, PhD projects differ tremendously both in their scope and circumstances, even within a single research institution.

Part 4: The Ugly¶

As mentioned previously, a PhD typically is a marathon-like long (4+ years) endeavour that typically comes with its fair share of long working hours, challenging experimental work where things often refuse to work as they should, tight deadlines on various submissions and long days in front of the screen filled with data processing and writing.

Meanwhile, your family and society will likely be (to a certain extent) questioning why are you working this hard for less money than non-academic jobs offer, and will also not understand why do you still have this “student” status stamped on your forehead while most of your fellow classmates from your undergrad already have “normal jobs”. They will wonder if you still have these perks of being a student such as summer holidays (no, you don’t) and how many exams you have to take this semester (typically, none). PhDs are stuck between two worlds, being typically full-time (aspiring) researchers, while simultaneously being considered “just students” by significant amount of people, which brings its fair share of misunderstanding of what PhD candidates actually do.

When you sum together all of the just mentioned factors, the duration of the project, very demanding workload with comparably lower financial compensation, the misunderstood societal status and the fact that being a PhD student means being a newbie among some very bright people (typically), a challenging environment ensues.

In the latest Nature survey, about 60% of PhD students claim that they are satisfied with their PhD experience. Unfortunately, some of the statistics related to the wellbeing and mental health of PhD students are straight-out ominous. “The days I spent pursuing my PhD in physics were some of my darkest.” states the author of the following article, and provides references that point out the abundance of mental health problems within the cohort of doctoral candidates. The Nature magazine is periodically releasing a survey on the experience of postgraduate students, and I believe it is certainly worth the read in its entirety rather than me paraphrasing excerpts from it - just to take a single figure out, 36% of students claim seeking professional help for anxiety or depression stemming from their PhD studies. Past studies claim that 47% of graduate students suffer from depression, and the so-called imposter syndrome is rampant among academics. On a different front, moving to a different country and bein an expat brings additional challenge to the already challenging PhD experience. The drop-out rate (people leaving their PhD without earning the degree) is also non-negligible, being estimated as high as 25%.

My intention is by no means to scare you, as a PhD can be a very rewarding, enriching experience - however, it is necessary to face the reality. What I personally find very encouraging is that more and more people are openly discussing these issues and lifting the stigma. By inviting these sensitive, but important issues into the public discource, we can make it clear that this is not an issue of individuals that are “outliers”, but rather an issue of the system as a whole. Despire what some may claim, there is no glory in working all the time and consistently pulling work-only Sundays.

The main reason I mention this within the scope of this article is to bring these facts to the attention to the prospective PhD candidates. Few things can do more damage than dealing with these common challenges in isolation, thinking that it’s one own fault or weakness, when the personal effects and hardships of these challenges represent a widespread, common problem. Always strive for a healthier work–life balance, and don’t be shy to talk about things and seek help, if you feel like you might need it. Nothing is worth losing one’s mental health, especially not a PhD.

In the second part of this write-up, I will focus more on my perspective regarding the overall value of obtaining a PhD, and probably also on some of the factors when considering academic vs. non-academic careers. Stay tuned! 😊

The Aftermath¶

Conventional wisdom says that PhD is a (necessary) step towards tenure-track career in academia (=becoming a professor), and therefore a necessity for people interested in such career direction. While I really did enjoy research I did at ICFO, I don’t come from academically-inclined family, and career in academia was not something I was particularly interested in. Therefore, one of the most pressing questions I was trying to figure back in those days was:

“Is doing a PhD worth it?” (from a longer-term career standpoint)

While the choice is rather clear for aspiring professors, the value of a PhD is much harder to properly evaluate for those that are more interested in non-academic careers. Are those (at least) 3-4 years in a PhD programme worth the sunk cost of not being actively participating in the non-academic workforce? Will there be someone outside of academia who will value the significant effort that an individual had to exhibit to earn their PhD? Are those (typically) late twenties worth staying in the ivory tower of academia instead of venturing to the real world and gaining experience there?

These are all significant questions that I was pondering at the time.

I believe one of the major challenges of trying to answer the question of generalized value of a PhD lies in the fact that the experience and potential pool of feature careers options differs dramatically between different fields of research.
